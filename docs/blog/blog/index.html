The dollar is a commodity <2026-04-22 Wed 15:27>

I'm reading a book called "What Has The Government Done to our Money" by Murray Rothbard. I learned this:

Money is a commodity. Its mostly demanded for its use as a medium for exchange.

Pre-money societies had barter systems. Every item (like tools, butter, milk) were considered marketable. A caveat to trade was that both parties coincidentally needed to want what each other had. Also, some bartered commodities were less divisible than others. For example, how can you determine how many eggs, flour, and butters a single snow plow is worth.

Indirect exchange was created. The two most used commodities in the USA are Silver and Gold. The free market has determined those to be the standards for barter. Money is a commodity just like those. The "price" of money is the exchange rate of a dollar to its weight in another commodity (gold or silver). The demand for the dollar is because of how easy it is to exchange for other goods. People buy dollars because dollars are easy to exchange for other goods and services. Its an easily divisible commodity, which we now call money.

That's about as much as I read which I can remark upon. I will be a millionaire!
